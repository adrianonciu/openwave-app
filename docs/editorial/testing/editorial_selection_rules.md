# OpenWave Editorial Selection Rules

## Core Principles
- Prioritize relevance over sensation.
- Prioritize citizen impact over institutional theater.
- Avoid bureaucratic language and policy phrasing that sounds written rather than spoken.
- Prefer stories that can be explained clearly in 3 to 5 sentences.
- Maintain topic diversity across the bulletin.
- Avoid stacking heavy political items back to back unless the news day requires it.
- Use quotes only when they improve voice, clarity, or immediacy.
- Use a Two Perspectives block only when there is real contrast that helps the listener understand the issue.
- Avoid redundant topics, even when multiple versions are available from different outlets.

## Selection Rules
- Build from unique event clusters, not from individual articles.
- Prefer stories with direct consequences for daily life, public services, money, mobility, health, education, safety, or major national decisions.
- Down-rank stories driven mainly by partisan exchange unless they produce a clear real-world effect.
- Down-rank items that require too much setup to work in a short audio bulletin.
- Keep the final list varied in tone, subject, and pace so the bulletin feels like a coherent radio product.

## Quote Attribution Rule
- Every quote must be attributed to a named speaker, an identifiable institution, or a media source.
- Quotes without attribution are not allowed in the briefing.
- If the source is journalistic rather than direct, the wording should make that clear for the editor before the line reaches audio.

## Actor Recognition Rule
- When a person is not widely known, introduce that person with a role or title.
- Prefer forms such as minister, mayor, analyst, economist, or spokesperson before the name when recognition is not guaranteed.
- The listener should understand why that person matters at the first mention.

## Title Actor Rule
- When the action is institutional, the title may refer to the institution rather than the individual.
- Use the institution in the title when it is more accurate or more recognizable than the officeholder's name.
- Use the individual in the title when the person is the real actor driving the decision or statement.

## Precision Rule
- Titles must name the correct economic, legal, or policy object.
- Do not simplify in a way that changes meaning.
- Prefer precise forms such as the price of oil, the budget deficit, or airport liquid rules instead of vague shorthand.

## Context Rule
- Add context in the title when ambiguity is possible.
- Use place names, policy names, or project names when they help the listener identify the story immediately.
- Prefer clear forms such as Autostrada Sibiu-Pitești instead of a generic reference to a motorway project.

## Expert Source Rule
- When official quotes are not available, expert quotes may be used to add interpretation or context.
- Expert use is strongest when the story involves security, economics, law, infrastructure, or other topics that benefit from explanation.
- Expert quotes should clarify the issue, not replace the underlying factual story.

## Working Hypothesis
### Bulletin Structure
- 8 standard stories
- 1 paired Two Perspectives block

### Story Length
- Standard story: 3 to 5 sentences
- Lead story: up to 6 sentences
- Maximum 1 quote per story
